The Cathedral of Theology

by J. V. Fesko · reviewed by Pulpit & Page

An ambitious prolegomena arguing that dogmatics should be built like a cathedral — ordered, beautiful, and ultimately for worship.

The Cathedral of Theology: Principles of Christian Dogmatics
J. V. Fesko · IVP Academic · Systematic Theology
Buy at AmazonChristianbook

Prolegomena — the questions of how we do theology before we do it — can read like throat-clearing. Fesko's wager is that method itself can be beautiful, and he organizes the whole enterprise around the image of a cathedral: a structure that is ordered and rational, yet built to lift the eyes upward to worship.

Strengths

This is a serious, confessionally Reformed account of theological method that takes Scripture, the creeds, and the Reformed tradition as load-bearing. Fesko is careful about the relationship between reason and revelation, and he resists the temptation to reduce dogmatics to either dry system or mere devotion.

Best read slowly, with your confession open beside it.

Where it asks a lot

It is dense in stretches, and readers new to prolegomena may want a systematic-theology survey at their elbow. This is not a first book on doctrine; it's a book about how the house of doctrine gets built.

The verdict

One of the more serious recent entries in confessional Reformed method. Seminarians, pastors, and theologically ambitious lay readers will be rewarded for the effort.
